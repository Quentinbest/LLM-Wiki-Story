# Genre Contract — The Third Day

## 1. The contract in one paragraph
This is a procedural insider story: an institutional employee discovers wrongdoing inside her own institution, and the story is the closing of the trap around her over a fixed window of time. The audience expects the granular texture of the institution's daily work (manifests, signatures, hierarchies), the slow accumulation of evidence, at least one scene where the protagonist could still walk away clean, a final moral choice that *cannot* be undone, and an ending where institutional power continues regardless of the choice. The audience does not expect courtroom catharsis; they expect a settling of irrevocable facts.

## 2. Primary genre
**Crime — Institutional/Political Drama (Insider-Discovers-Insider subgenre).** It is *crime* because a real crime is at the spine and is not abstracted; *institutional/political* because the criminal is the institution; *Insider-Discovers-Insider* because the discoverer is *also* employed by the same institution and therefore cannot use the institution to act on her discovery. The subgenre's defining feature: the protagonist's professional role *is* her tool and her cage.

## 3. Secondary genre
**Punitive Plot** (in the McKee taxonomy: a good person becomes bad and ends punished). Yu Min begins idealistic and ends corrupted; the punishment is not legal but ontological — she has become the thing she was meant to oppose. The Punitive frame must remain *secondary*: if it dominates, the story tilts moralistic, which the ironic Controlling Idea cannot survive.

## 4. Conventions (the audience expects)

- [primary] **The institutional everyday on the page**: manifests, log books, the inspector's clipboard, the chain of seals. The work is real, not gestural.
- [primary] **Hierarchies named and visible**: who reports to whom; who can be ignored; who can be displeased.
- [primary] **One ally who knows just enough to be dangerous** (peer inspector, dock worker, clerk).
- [primary] **A scene of small corruption normalized** before the big corruption is discovered — the audience must understand the institution's grammar.
- [primary] **A clock**: a posting decision, a court date, a deadline that cannot be moved.
- [primary] **At least one private confrontation** between the protagonist and the wrongdoer, where the audience sees both as moral beings.
- [primary] **Surveillance texture** (1989 setting amplifies this): a sense that being watched is the default.
- [secondary] **A late-act collapse of the protagonist's moral baseline** (Punitive convention).
- [shared] **Final-image continuity**: the institution outlasts the protagonist.

## 5. Obligatory scenes (the audience came for)

1. **The Discovery** *(Act 1 close)* — Yu Min sees, unambiguously, the manifest discrepancy that points to Wei. The audience must see what she sees, in detail; if we are told but not shown, the contract breaks.
2. **The Private Confrontation** *(mid Act 2)* — Yu Min and Wei, alone, the truth between them. He neither denies nor pleads; she cannot extract the dialogue she came for.
3. **The Offered Escape** *(late Act 2)* — Yu Min is given a clean way out (transfer request, pliable evidence, an outside investigator who would take her statement). She refuses or fails to take it. The audience must feel the refusal as a *choice*, not a circumstance.
4. **The Crisis Moment of Recognition** *(Act 3)* — Yu Min sees that the only way to honor Wei is to take the corruption onto herself. This must be a single beat — a held image, not a deliberation.
5. **The Climax: the seal lands** *(Act 3)* — she files the falsified report under her own name. This is the obligatory scene of the *whole story*, the moment the audience came for. It must occur on screen/page; off-page Climax is forbidden.
6. **The Replication Image** *(Resolution)* — the corruption visibly continues through her. (Often: she initials a younger inspector's clean report.)

## 6. Exemplars

- ***A Touch of Sin*** (天注定, 2013, dir. Jia Zhangke) — institutional inertia and the individual swallowed by it; the modern Chinese reference for this subgenre.
- ***The Lives of Others*** (2006) — insider as discoverer; the moral seriousness of the watched and the watcher; the terminal moral choice that perpetuates a system.
- ***Chinatown*** (1974) — the irony of *not* prevailing; the gold standard for ironic Controlling Ideas in crime.
- ***Dog Day Afternoon*** (1975) — institutional procedural texture; how to keep the audience inside small physical spaces for a long time.
- ***A Hero*** (Ghahreman, 2021) — the tightening trap; how social and institutional pressure together collapse a single moral choice.

## 7. Subgenre conventions (Insider-Discovers-Insider)

- The protagonist's *professional credentials* are simultaneously the means of discovery and the means of complicity. Cannot be a journalist or an outsider.
- The *investigation tools* are the protagonist's job tools (manifests, ledgers, internal review) — not interrogation scenes.
- The *witness* role is double-bound: speaking risks self-incrimination; silence risks being implicated by association.
- A *peer* must exist who has already made the corrupt choice — to show what Yu Min could become.

## 8. Anti-conventions (planned inversions)

| Convention | Planned inversion | Cost / risk | Compensation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Crime stories climax in revelation | Our Climax is *concealment* — the protagonist files the false report. | Loss of the genre's classic catharsis. Audience may feel cheated of "justice." | We compensate by making the concealment *legible* — every detail of the false signature is shown; the audience reads the fall in real time. The Resolution's replication image then deepens, rather than dissipates, the missing catharsis. |
| Corrupt mentor exposed | Our mentor is *protected* — Wei is preserved by the protagonist's act, not exposed by it. | Risk of moral confusion: the audience may read this as endorsement of the corruption. | The Resolution image (the next inspector) shows the cost of the protection so plainly that no thoughtful viewer can read it as endorsement. The Idea lives in the irony. |
| Institutional drama ends with reform or with martyrdom | Our story ends with *replication* — the system absorbs Yu Min and continues. | Audience may feel structurally untreated. | This is the ironic ending the Controlling Idea demands; we cannot honor the genre's reform/martyrdom conventions without breaking the Idea. The risk is acceptable; we lean into it. |

## 9. What this contract forbids

1. Cannot resolve via outside investigator.
2. Cannot resolve in a courtroom.
3. Cannot give the audience a "this matters" public reckoning.
4. Cannot let Wei be reduced to a villain.
5. Cannot end on Yu Min's resignation — that would be martyrdom; we need replication.
6. Cannot let the racket be a faceless force; one named broker (or shipment captain) must give it embodiment.
7. Cannot extend the three-day clock.

## 11. Open questions for the writer
- Should the racket-broker be a named on-screen character, or a voice on a phone? (Embodied is stronger but adds cast weight.)
- The 1989 setting carries political-historical charge — do we want the autumn surveillance climate to *act on* the spine, or remain texture?
- Can we afford a peer-inspector role to embody "what Yu Min could become"? (Probably yes; flag for `cast-balancer`.)

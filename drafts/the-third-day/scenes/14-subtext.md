# Subtext — Scene 14: The Endorsement

## 1. Frame

- **POV / participants**: Yu Min (POV); Supervisor Wei.
- **Scene objective (text-level)**: Yu Min wants to extract acknowledgement of the racket from Wei without naming it, so she can file the truthful report without ruining him.
- **POV's unconscious need (from Character File)**: To be free of the saving-story — to no longer be the daughter Wei rescued.
- **Counterpart's hidden agenda**: Wei wants to give Yu Min the endorsement *without* giving her any other thing — not denial, not confession, not absolution. He is letting her decide, and the giving-without-asking is itself his moral position.
- **Named value of the scene**: integrity / complicity (alt: moral high ground / moral debt).

## 3. On-the-nose line list

### Beat 6b — Wei's *"Whatever you decide, I will sign it."*

- **Why it fails**: it names the choice that the entire scene has refused to name. The whole architecture of the scene is that Wei does *not* speak to the choice — he *enacts* it. This line breaks the architecture. It also collapses subtext into text: he is doing exactly what he is saying. McKee's failure mode 2 (subtext == desire becomes "preachy or therapized").
- **Genre exception?** No — institutional drama earns *some* direct statement at high-stakes events (verdicts, confessions). This is neither. The act of placing the endorsement letter has already done the moral work; the line gilds.
- **Subtext to preserve**: he is binding himself to her decision *in advance*; he wants her to know that his protection is unconditional and therefore not a pressure.
- **Rewrite directions** (writer chooses prose):
  - **Action route (preferred)**: cut the line entirely. Replace with a beat: *Wei does not look up as she reaches the door. He picks up his pen and crosses something off a list on his desk — possibly a name, possibly a date. The crossing-off is the binding gesture; we do not need to read what he crosses.* The audience receives "I am bound to your decision" through *his calmness about her departure.*
  - **Indirect-line route**: replace with a procedural line that *contains* the binding without naming it. *"There's a second copy on my desk. If the first is misfiled, the second is identical."* The audience hears: he has anticipated; he is offering cover; he has made his decision survive any of hers.
  - **Silent route**: cut beat 6b entirely. The scene ends with beat 5's gesture and beat 6a's name. Beat 6c becomes Yu Min's exit. *Risk: the scene loses its final moral signature; weighing against: McKee's discipline favors the cut.*

**Recommendation**: **action route**. The crossed-off line on Wei's desk is concrete, paintable, and gives `key-image-curator` a hook (paper-and-pen image system already running through the manifest signatures).

### Beat 6a — Wei's *"敏敏."*

- **Why it's borderline, not on-the-nose**: the diminutive is technically a *naming* — but it is one syllable, not a sentence; it carries weight by *contrast* with the entire scene's formality, not by stating an emotion or want. McKee's *naming-the-feeling* failure requires a feeling-noun or an emotion-verb; "敏敏" is a vocative.
- **However**: the line risks reading as sentimental in execution. If the actor or the prose lingers on it, it tips on-the-nose.
- **Recommendation**: **keep — but stage as throwaway**. Wei says it without looking at her. It is the acoustic fact of her childhood name being said in this office, not the gesture of his face. The line lands by being almost-missed.

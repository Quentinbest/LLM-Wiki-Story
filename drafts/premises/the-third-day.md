# Premise Slate — The Third Day

## 0. The raw input

> *A young customs inspector at the Dalian port, 1989, discovers her supervisor — the man whose intervention kept her family alive during the Cultural Revolution — is running a smuggling-protection racket. Her permanent posting decision is in three days.*

Detected approach: **outside-in**. The user starts with a world (Dalian port, 1989), an event (the discovery), and a tight clock (three days). The interior — what *kind* of person Yu Min is — is still open. The work of these candidates is to find the protagonist whose specific contradictions this world will rupture most productively.

## 1. Candidates

### Candidate 1 — *The Inheritor*

- **What if**: *What if a young customs inspector, raised on the story of the supervisor who saved her family, must choose between completing the report that will destroy him or burning it and inheriting his racket?*
- **Protagonist**: Yu Min, 24, the inspector's daughter generation; raised on the saving-story; recently certified, idealistic but already tempted by the small corruptions she has watched her senior colleagues normalize.
- **Inciting Incident (provisional)**: She intercepts a manifest she was not meant to see; the discrepancy points unambiguously to her supervisor.
- **Object of Desire**: *Conscious*: to file a clean report and earn the permanent posting. *Unconscious*: to be free of the debt her family owes him.
- **Top three Forces of Antagonism**:
  - *Extra-personal*: the customs bureau hierarchy; the smuggling network; the political climate (1989: protests in the spring, surveillance tightening in autumn).
  - *Personal*: her parents, who learn what she has found and beg her to bury it; the supervisor himself, who *will not* let her destroy herself for him.
  - *Inner*: gratitude curdled into entrapment; her own appetite for the path the supervisor offers.
- **Probable genre**: **Crime — institutional/political drama**, with a strong **redemption** secondary (the supervisor's, not the protagonist's).
- **Probable Controlling-Idea pole**: **ironic** — gratitude ruins a soul that could have been saved by ingratitude. *(Or, alt-reading: ingratitude saves a soul gratitude would have ruined.)*
- **Probable setting**: Dalian port and the inspector's housing block, late September 1989, three days, narrow.
- **What this premise forbids**:
  - Cannot end on a clean win — gratitude is the antagonist.
  - Cannot resolve via outside agency (no inspector-from-Beijing arriving in Act 3) — the dilemma is hers.
  - Cannot frame the supervisor as villain; he must be *credibly worth saving*.
- **Six-Point Audit**: ✅ concrete protagonist · ✅ specific event · ✅ Object of Desire ✅ both layers · ✅ real antagonism · ✅ specific enough to forbid things · ✅ has a question (will she pay the debt by destroying herself, or by destroying him?)
- **Why this candidate exists**: explores the **inner** axis hardest; idealist Idea pole inverted to ironic.

### Candidate 2 — *The Witness*

- **What if**: *What if a young customs inspector, the only one her supervisor has refused to corrupt because he loved her late mother, discovers the racket through an outside informant — a smuggler who wants her to bring the supervisor down, and offers her enough evidence to do it?*
- **Protagonist**: Yu Min, 24, raised by the supervisor after her mother's death; emotionally formal, professionally meticulous, has never asked the questions she should have.
- **Inciting Incident**: A young dock worker she has flagged in a previous inspection corners her at a noodle stall and slides a ledger across the table.
- **Object of Desire**: *Conscious*: to verify the truth before she acts. *Unconscious*: to know whether the man who loved her mother *is* worth the love she has given him.
- **Top three Forces of Antagonism**:
  - *Extra-personal*: the smuggler-informant has his own agenda and will manipulate her; the bureau will read her hesitation as complicity.
  - *Personal*: the supervisor; her aunt who depends on the supervisor's stipend.
  - *Inner*: she has built her identity on his approval.
- **Probable genre**: **Crime — informer/courtroom thriller**, secondary **maturation** (the protagonist coming into adult judgment).
- **Probable Controlling-Idea pole**: **idealist** — truth prevails because the protagonist learns to see clearly; but the cost is high. (Could pivot pessimist if the informant betrays her in Act 3.)
- **Probable setting**: as above; tighter — the supervisor's apartment, the noodle stall, the customs office.
- **What this premise forbids**:
  - Cannot ignore the informant's agenda — he is not a tool.
  - Cannot resolve in court — Yu Min has no court access; the spine must resolve in private confrontation.
- **Six-Point Audit**: ✅ × 6.
- **Why this candidate exists**: explores the **personal** axis hardest; classical idealist pole.

### Candidate 3 — *The Inheritor's Sister*

- **What if**: *What if a young customs inspector's older brother — also at customs — is the one feeding the supervisor's racket, and her discovery threatens not the supervisor but her brother, who has been sending the family's only income home?*
- **Protagonist**: Yu Min, 24, the younger sibling; the bookish one; the brother is the family hero who pulled them out of poverty.
- **Inciting Incident**: A discrepancy she expected to disprove, doesn't.
- **Object of Desire**: *Conscious*: to clear her brother. *Unconscious*: to stop being the family's grateful little sister.
- **Top three Forces of Antagonism**:
  - *Extra-personal*: customs bureau; the network the brother fronts for.
  - *Personal*: the brother; the parents who will not survive losing him.
  - *Inner*: she has spent her life under the brother's debt.
- **Probable genre**: **Crime — domestic drama**, secondary **disillusionment**.
- **Probable Controlling-Idea pole**: **pessimist** — family destroys what truth could have saved.
- **Probable setting**: as above, with the family apartment as third location.
- **What this premise forbids**: cannot end happily; cannot resolve without the family's complicity surfacing.
- **Six-Point Audit**: ✅ × 6.
- **Why this candidate exists**: explores the **personal** axis at the family level (different from C2's mentor-relationship); pessimist Idea pole.

## 2. Recommendation

> **Lead candidate**: **Candidate 1 — The Inheritor.**
>
> **Reason**: it builds the deepest Crisis. The supervisor as antagonist *and* benefactor produces an irreducible dilemma — she cannot honor gratitude without destroying her professional self, and she cannot serve her professional self without unmaking the family's saving-story. Candidates 2 and 3 produce hard choices but each has a tilt toward one horn. C1 also positions the Controlling Idea where it can land ironically (gratitude as ruin), which is the strongest value the premise's specifics support — the political year and the institutional trap make a clean idealist redemption read as naïve.
>
> **Strong runner-up**: **Candidate 2 — The Witness** — keep alive in case the user reaches the controlling-idea phase and finds the ironic pole too dark for the work they want to write.

## 3. Open questions for the writer
- Is the supervisor permitted to *survive* the climax, or must the spine deliver his fall?
- Is Yu Min's family aware of the supervisor's debt, or is she alone in carrying that knowledge?
- Does the political-historical context (autumn 1989, post-spring) act on the spine, or is it backdrop?
- Should the smuggling network be a faceless force or an embodied antagonist (a named broker)?
- One-sentence preference on ending tone: ironic / pessimist / idealist?

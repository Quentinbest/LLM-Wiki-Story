# No Scene That Doesn't Turn

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/principles/no-scene-that-doesnt-turn|中文]]

## The Principle

Every scene must turn the value-charged condition of a character's life. If the value at the end of a scene is the same as at the beginning, nothing meaningful has happened—the scene is a nonevent and should be cut.

## McKee's Reasoning

McKee states this as his ideal for screenwriting. A scene exists to create change. If no value shifts, the scene's only justification is likely exposition—conveying information to the audience. A disciplined writer will trash such scenes and weave their information elsewhere. The principle applies universally across genres: Action genres turn on public values (freedom/slavery); Education genres turn on interior values (self-awareness/self-deception).

## In Practice

For every scene: (1) Note the value at stake and its charge at the opening. (2) Note the charge at the close. (3) If unchanged, ask why the scene exists. (4) If the answer is "exposition," cut the scene and redistribute its information. This discipline is difficult but not impossible—films like *Die Hard*, *The Fugitive*, *Remains of the Day*, and *The Accidental Tourist* all meet this test.

## Film Examples

- **[[tender-mercies]]** — Every scene subtly but decisively turns a value in Sledge's inner journey
- *Die Hard*, *The Fugitive*, *Straw Dogs* — Clearly meet the test with external value turns
- *Remains of the Day*, *The Accidental Tourist* — Meet the test with subtler internal value turns

## Violations and Consequences

Scenes that don't turn produce a "lifeless collection of predictable, ill-told, and clichéd episodes" (as McKee describes in his typical coverage report). The story stalls, the audience loses interest, and the work degenerates into either static portraiture or empty spectacle.

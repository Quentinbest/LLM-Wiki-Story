# Dramatize, Don't Explain

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/principles/dramatize-dont-explain|中文]]

## The Principle

Master storytellers never explain. They dramatize. Storytelling is the creative demonstration of truth — a story is the living proof of an idea, the conversion of idea to action. A story's event structure is the means by which the writer first expresses, then proves his idea — without explanation.

## McKee's Reasoning

Audiences are rarely interested, and certainly never convinced, when forced to listen to the discussion of ideas. Dialogue is not a platform for the filmmaker's philosophy. A great story authenticates its ideas solely within the dynamics of its events. Failure to express a view of life through the pure, honest consequences of human choice and action is "a creative defeat no amount of clever language can salvage."

The story's meaning — whether comic or tragic — must be dramatized in an emotionally expressive Story Climax without the aid of explanatory dialogue.

## In Practice

- Express your Controlling Idea through events, not speeches
- Build the dialectic of [[idea-vs-counter-idea]] through dramatized sequences, not philosophical discussions
- Trust the audience to grasp meaning through action and consequence
- If a character must explain the theme, the events haven't done their job

## Film Examples

- The Crime genre demonstrates this perfectly: "Crime doesn't pay" is never stated — it's proven when the criminal is caught and punished, sending "They got the bastard!" through the audience.

## Violations and Consequences

- **Didacticism** — Zealous preaching through on-the-nose dialogue kills art. Antiwar films that simply show war's horrors while characters lament its insanity rarely convince, because they're blind to the truth that "men love war."
- Kubrick's antiwar trilogy succeeds precisely because it *dramatizes* the Counter-Idea — humanity's love of violence — rather than explaining why war is bad.

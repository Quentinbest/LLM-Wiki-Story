# Craft Maximizes Talent

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/principles/craft-maximizes-talent|中文]]

## The Principle

Talent without craft is like fuel without an engine. It burns wildly but accomplishes nothing. Only by using everything you know about the craft of storytelling can you make your talent forge story.

## McKee's Reasoning

Craft is not mechanics or gimmicks—it is "the concert of techniques by which we create a conspiracy of interest between ourselves and the audience." It is the sum total of all means used to draw the audience into deep involvement, hold that involvement, and reward it with a moving and meaningful experience.

Without craft, the writer sits helpless before his own work, unable to answer: "Is it good? Or is it sewage? If sewage, what do I do?" The conscious mind, fixated on these terrible questions, blocks the subconscious. But when the conscious mind is put to work on the objective task of executing craft, the spontaneous surfaces. "Mastery of craft frees the subconscious."

## In Practice

A writer's daily rhythm: enter the imagined world, write as characters speak and act, then step out and read analytically. "You write, you read; create, critique; impulse, logic; right brain, left brain; re-imagine, rewrite." The quality of rewriting depends on command of craft. An artist willfully exercises craft to create harmonies of instinct and idea.

## Film Examples

- The principle is illustrated by contrast: a gifted raconteur who can make "How I Put My Kids on the School Bus" riveting vs. a man with a genuinely heartbreaking story (his mother's death) who bores everyone with surface-level, cliché telling.
- "Given the choice between trivial material brilliantly told versus profound material badly told, an audience will always choose the trivial told brilliantly."

## Violations and Consequences

Writers who rely solely on "instinct" (actually unconscious absorption of patterns) produce work that is "rigidly limiting"—either imitating their mental prototype or rebelling against it, producing clichés of the commercial or art-house variety.

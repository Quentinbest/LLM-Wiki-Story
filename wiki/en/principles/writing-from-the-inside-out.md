# Writing from the Inside Out

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/principles/writing-from-the-inside-out|中文]]

## The Principle
**Write from the inside out:** begin with the character's inner life — desire, expectation, emotion under pressure — and let the outer scene (dialogue, setting, action) emerge as the inevitable expression of that inner reality.

## McKee's Reasoning
Writers who work "outside in" — starting from clever dialogue, visual ideas, or plot mechanics — produce hollow scenes because they have skipped the step of knowing what the character *really* wants moment to moment. Writing from the inside out forces you to enter the character, find what they would do to close [[the-gap]], and then discover the scene that expresses it.

```mermaid
flowchart LR
  inner([Inner Life])
  desire[Desire]
  gap[The Gap]
  action[Outer Action]
  scene[Scene]
  inner --> desire
  desire --> gap
  gap --> action
  action --> scene
```

## In Practice
1. Identify what the character wants in this scene, and what they expect will close the gap.
2. Feel, as the character, the friction between expectation and reality.
3. Let that friction produce the scene's action — dialogue follows last.

## Film Examples
- *Chinatown* — Towne writes Gittes from inside his wound, so every line of banter carries hidden ache.
- *Ordinary People* — Conrad's silences are written from inside his guilt, not from outside "awkwardness."

## Violations and Consequences
- On-the-nose dialogue that states what should be subtext.
- Scenes that feel clever but empty — the outside-in signature.
- Plot moving characters rather than character-choice producing plot.

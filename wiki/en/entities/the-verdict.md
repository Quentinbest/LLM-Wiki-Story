# The Verdict (1982)

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/entities/the-verdict|中文]]

## Overview

A disgraced, alcoholic Boston attorney, Frank Galvin (Paul Newman), is offered a medical malpractice settlement that would net him an easy $70,000. But when he sees his comatose client, he recognizes the case as his last chance for redemption and chooses to fight the Catholic Church and the political establishment — not just for his client, but for his own soul.

## McKee's Usage

McKee uses *The Verdict* as his primary example of [[character-arc]] in Chapter 5. He traces the full arc:

1. **Characterization:** Handsome Boston attorney, looking like Paul Newman — "unfairly handsome"
2. **True character revealed:** Corrupt, bankrupt, self-destructive drunk who hasn't won a case in years, trolling funerals for insurance litigation, culminating in a drunken rage where he trashes his own office
3. **The turning point:** He looks at his helpless comatose client and senses this case offers not easy money but his last chance for salvation
4. **The choice:** He takes on the Catholic Church and political establishment
5. **The arc completed:** Victory resurrects him into a sober, ethical, excellent attorney — the kind of man he once was

## Concepts Illustrated

- **[[character-arc]]** — One of cinema's great redemption arcs: from self-destruction to salvation through moral choice
- **[[characterization-vs-true-character]]** — The handsome exterior masks a ruined man; the ruined man conceals a fighter who will risk everything
- **[[character-revelation]]** — Multiple layers peeled back: surface (handsome lawyer) → first revelation (corrupt drunk) → deeper revelation (man willing to fight for his soul)

## Key Scenes Analyzed

McKee highlights the sequence where Galvin trolls funerals to hand out business cards, culminating in the drunken destruction of his office — the lowest point that makes his subsequent choice to fight all the more powerful.

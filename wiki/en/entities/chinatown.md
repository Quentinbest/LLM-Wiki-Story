# Chinatown (1974)

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/entities/chinatown|中文]]

## Overview

Roman Polanski's neo-noir masterpiece, written by Robert Towne, follows private detective Jake Gittes as he uncovers a vast conspiracy involving water rights, political corruption, and incest in 1930s Los Angeles. The film ends with the villain escaping justice and the innocent victim destroyed.

## McKee's Usage

McKee references *Chinatown* across multiple chapters as one of cinema's supreme achievements — a work that demonstrates absolute command of genre while breaking its most honored convention.

In Chapter 3, McKee uses the film as an example of a specific, deeply researched setting — 1930s LA is rendered with such commanding knowledge that every detail feels inevitable.

In Chapter 4, *Chinatown* is McKee's central example of genre reinvention. In every previous Murder Mystery, the detective apprehends and punishes the criminal. Towne and Polanski broke this convention — the wealthy, politically powerful killer gets away with murder. This was only possible in the 1970s, when Watergate, the Vietnam War, and the civil rights movement had awakened America to the depth of its corruption. The film rewrote the Crime genre, opening the door to down-ending crime stories like *Body Heat*, *Crimes and Misdemeanors*, and *Seven*.

McKee calls it a classic — "reexperienced with pleasure because it can be reinterpreted through the decades, because in it truth and humanity are so abundant that each new generation finds itself mirrored in the story."

## Key Scenes Analyzed

McKee highlights the climax: unlike every previous Murder Mystery, the criminal escapes and the innocent are destroyed. "Forget it, Jake — it's Chinatown" became the genre's epitaph and rebirth.

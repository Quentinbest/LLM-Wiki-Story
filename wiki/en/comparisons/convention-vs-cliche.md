# Convention vs. Cliché

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/comparisons/convention-vs-cliche|中文]]

## Overview

One of McKee's most critical distinctions: convention and cliché are not the same thing. Confusing them leads writers either to reject genre conventions (producing formless work) or to reproduce tired patterns (producing boring work). Understanding the difference is essential to writing within genre with originality.

## Key Differences

| Dimension | Convention | Cliché |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | A necessary element of genre form | A stale execution of that element |
| Level | *What* must happen | *How* it's been done before |
| Audience reaction | Satisfaction (expectation fulfilled) | Boredom (seen it before) |
| Writer's task | Must fulfill it | Must avoid it |
| Example | Boy meets girl in a Love Story | They meet in a singles bar... again |
| Relationship to genre | Defines the genre | Degrades the genre |
| Origin | Audience expectation built over centuries | Lazy borrowing from previous works |

## McKee's Position

McKee is emphatic: conventions are non-negotiable. Omitting a convention confuses the audience. The challenge is exclusively in execution — finding fresh, original ways to fulfill what the audience expects. "The challenge is to keep convention but avoid cliché."

He compares this to poetry: a rhyme scheme (convention) forces the poet to find unexpected words, which in turn create unexpected meaning. The constraint itself is the engine of originality. Without the net, tennis becomes meaningless; without conventions, genre becomes formless.

## Film Examples

- **Convention exemplified by:** [[chinatown]] — fulfills the Murder Mystery convention of "solving the crime" but with a devastating twist: the detective solves it but can't punish the criminal
- **Cliché exemplified by:** The tired "hero-at-the-mercy-of-the-villain" where the villain monologues instead of killing — versus the fresh execution in *Die Hard* (gun taped to naked back) or *Raiders of the Lost Ark* (just shoot the swordsman)

## Synthesis

The convention/cliché distinction reveals McKee's core philosophy of craft: mastery lies not in rejecting form but in finding new life within it. The writer who avoids conventions is not more creative than the one who fulfills them freshly — they are simply ignorant of their genre. True originality is convention fulfilled in a way no one has seen before.

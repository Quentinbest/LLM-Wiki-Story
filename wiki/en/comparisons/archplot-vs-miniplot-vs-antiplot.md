# Archplot vs. Miniplot vs. Antiplot

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/comparisons/archplot-vs-miniplot-vs-antiplot|中文]]

## Overview

McKee's [[the-story-triangle]] maps the three fundamental approaches to story design. Understanding how they differ—and what they share—is essential for every writer.

## Key Differences

| Dimension | [[archplot]] | [[miniplot]] | [[antiplot]] |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ending | Closed | Open | Open or circular |
| Conflict emphasis | External | Internal | Fragmented |
| Protagonist | Single, active | Passive or multiple | Variable |
| Time | Linear | Linear | Nonlinear |
| Causality | Causal chains | Causal (reduced) | Coincidence |
| Reality | Consistent | Consistent | Inconsistent |
| Audience size | Largest | Moderate | Smallest |

## McKee's Position

McKee does not declare one form superior. All three corners "gleam with masterworks that the world treasures." However, he argues that the Archplot is the form most aligned with how humans naturally perceive reality and therefore commands the largest audience. He insists writers must [[master-classical-form]] before venturing into minimalism or antistructure, and must write from genuine conviction, not political posturing.

## Film Examples

- **Archplot:** *Chinatown*, *The Seven Samurai*, *Citizen Kane*, *Shine*
- **Miniplot:** *Tender Mercies*, *Paris Texas*, *The Accidental Tourist*, *Pelle the Conqueror*
- **Antiplot:** *After Hours*, *Weekend*, *Monty Python and the Holy Grail*, *Bad Timing*
- **Blends:** *Barton Fink* (center), *The Crying Game* (Arch-Mini), *When Harry Met Sally* (quasi-Anti)

## Synthesis

The three forms are not isolated camps but a continuous spectrum. Most films blend elements from multiple points on the triangle. The critical insight is that Miniplot *reduces* the Archplot while Antiplot *reverses* it—both are defined in relation to Classical Design. Writers who understand this relationship can make informed, authentic choices about where their stories live.

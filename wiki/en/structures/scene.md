# Scene

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/structures/scene|中文]]

## Definition

A Scene is an action through conflict in more or less continuous time and space that turns the value-charged condition of a character's life on at least one value with a degree of perceptible significance. Ideally, every scene is a [[story-event]].

## Position in the Story Hierarchy

- **Above:** [[sequence]] — Scenes build sequences; a capping scene delivers the sequence climax
- **Below:** [[beat]] — Beats compose scenes through exchanges of action/reaction
- **This level:** An action through conflict that turns at least one value in a character's life

## McKee's Argument

The scene is the fundamental building block of story. McKee argues that a typical film contains forty to sixty scenes. Each must turn a [[story-values|value]]—if the value-charged condition at the end of a scene is the same as at the beginning, "nothing meaningful happens" and the scene is a nonevent, likely serving only as exposition. McKee's principle [[no-scene-that-doesnt-turn]] demands that every scene create meaningful change.

## How It Works

To evaluate a scene: (1) Identify the value at stake at the opening—positive or negative? (2) Identify the value at the close—has it changed? (3) If unchanged, the scene is likely exposition and should be cut or its information woven elsewhere. A scene may shift across multiple locations (bedroom → kitchen → highway) but remains one scene if it constitutes a single story event.

## Film Examples

- McKee's "lovers break up" example: Four location shifts (bedroom, kitchen, garage, highway) form one scene because a single value turns—the couple goes from together/in love to apart/in hate at the Turning Point when Andy slams the car door
- **[[tender-mercies]]** — Every scene subtly turns values in Sledge's inner journey from meaninglessness to meaning

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[beat]] — Beats are the internal building blocks of scenes
- [[sequence]] — Scenes build into sequences; the capping scene is the Sequence Climax
- [[story-values]] — Each scene must turn at least one value
- [[story-event]] — Ideally, every scene is a story event
- [[no-scene-that-doesnt-turn]] — The guiding principle for scene design

## Common Mistakes

McKee warns against scenes whose only purpose is exposition—conveying information without changing values. He also warns against confusing camera setups or location changes with true scenes. A series of locations showing an escalating argument is one scene, not four.

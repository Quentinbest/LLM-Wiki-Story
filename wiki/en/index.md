## Concepts
- [[structure]] — The internal system of events building to a climax expressing a specific meaning
- [[story-as-metaphor]] — Story as the essential equipment for living, a metaphor for life
- [[story-values]] — The universal qualities of human experience that shift through storytelling
- [[story-event]] — A meaningful change in a character's life situation expressed through value shifts
- [[story-form]] — The spectrum of narrative forms from pure event-driven to pure non-event cinema
- [[the-story-triangle]] — The three-cornered design map: Archplot, Miniplot, and Antiplot
- [[setting]] — The four-dimensional world of a story: Period, Duration, Location, Level of Conflict
- [[creative-limitation]] — The principle that constraints inspire rather than inhibit creativity
- [[research]] — Three methods of acquiring story knowledge: memory, imagination, and fact
- [[creative-choices]] — The discipline of overproduction and rigorous selection
- [[genre]] — The 25 film genres classified by subject, setting, role, event, and values
- [[genre-conventions]] — The specific settings, roles, events, and values that define individual genres
- [[mixing-genres]] — Combining genres to create richer meaning and emotional variety
- [[aesthetic-emotion]] — The simultaneous encounter of thought and feeling that art creates
- [[premise]] — The open-ended "What if...?" that inspires the creative process
- [[controlling-idea]] — A story's irreducible meaning: Value + Cause, expressed through the climax
- [[idea-vs-counter-idea]] — The dialectical engine: positive and negative charges arguing to climax
- [[the-gap]] — The chasm between expectation and result that every action opens
- [[inciting-incident]] — The event that radically upsets the protagonist's life balance
- [[progressive-complications]] — The rising wave of complication that carries the story to crisis
- [[points-of-no-return]] — Irrevocable decisions that commit the protagonist deeper into the story
- [[spine]] — The line of the protagonist's pursuit of desire
- [[object-of-desire]] — The specific thing, concrete or abstract, the protagonist pursues
- [[the-quest]] — The archetypal shape underlying every story: a protagonist after an object of desire
- [[major-dramatic-question]] — The central "will-they?" question raised at the inciting incident
- [[obligatory-scene]] — The climactic scene the audience has been primed to demand
- [[foreshadowing]] — The placement of future-paying information
- [[risk]] — What the protagonist stands to lose if desire fails
- [[action-vs-activity]] — True action changes value; activity does not
- [[levels-of-conflict]] — Inner, personal, and extra-personal planes of antagonism
- [[minimum-conservative-action]] — The smallest, least costly action a character takes to achieve desire
- [[authenticity]] — The inner consistency that earns suspension of disbelief
- [[cast-design]] — The deliberate polarization of the ensemble around the protagonist
- [[backstory]] — Significant past events the writer will later harvest
- [[false-ending]] — A late scene of apparent closure reversed by the true climax
- [[act-rhythm]] — The patterned escalation of act climaxes so none repeats its predecessor's charge
- [[turning-point]] — The gap-driven reversal that shifts value and redirects the story
- [[scene-objective]] — The immediate desire pursued in a specific scene
- [[setup-and-payoff]] — Planted knowledge that later turns and revelations cash in
- [[text-and-subtext]] — The visible surface of a scene and the concealed life beneath it
- [[crisis]] — The protagonist's final decision under maximum pressure
- [[dilemma]] — True dramatic choice: irreconcilable goods or the lesser of two evils
- [[key-image]] — The final image that concentrates a story's meaning and emotion
- [[resolution]] — The post-climax material that settles what is left
- [[unity-and-variety]] — Causal unity combined with internal freshness and contrast
- [[pacing]] — The modulation of tension and release across the story
- [[symbolic-ascension]] — The rise from literal image to archetypal charge
- [[principle-of-transition]] — The hinge that links one scene to the next

## Structures
- [[beat]] — The smallest unit of story structure: an exchange of action/reaction
- [[scene]] — A unit of story built from beats, turning on a value shift through conflict
- [[sequence]] — A series of 2–5 scenes building to a moderate reversal
- [[act]] — A series of sequences climaxing in a major turning point
- [[story-climax]] — The final, irreversible change that answers the story's major dramatic question
- [[archplot]] — Classical Design: single protagonist, active, causally linked, closed ending
- [[miniplot]] — Minimalism: reduced narrative elements while retaining enough to engage
- [[antiplot]] — Anti-structure: deliberately reverses classical principles
- [[subplot]] — Secondary story line that contradicts, resonates, complicates, or sets up the central plot

## Principles
- [[no-scene-that-doesnt-turn]] — Every scene must contain a value change or it doesn't belong
- [[master-classical-form]] — Writers must master Archplot before experimenting with alternatives
- [[tell-story]] — McKee's imperative: the writer's first duty is to tell a story
- [[craft-maximizes-talent]] — Craft is the means to free creative talent, not restrict it
- [[war-on-cliche]] — The root of all cliché is ignorance of the story's world
- [[story-obeys-its-world]] — A story must obey its own internal laws of probability
- [[master-your-genre]] — Writers must master genre conventions to anticipate audience expectations
- [[structure-is-character]] — Structure is character; character is structure — they are the same thing
- [[dramatize-dont-explain]] — Master storytellers dramatize; they never explain
- [[tell-the-truth]] — The artist's only responsibility is to tell the truth
- [[law-of-conflict]] — Nothing moves forward in story except through conflict
- [[writing-from-the-inside-out]] — Begin from the character's inner life; let outer scene follow
- [[meaning-produces-emotion]] — Emotion at climax comes from meaningful value change
- [[inevitable-and-unexpected]] — The ending must feel both necessary and surprising

## Entities (Films & Works)
- [[tender-mercies]] — *Tender Mercies* (1983): McKee's example of minimalist storytelling and Miniplot
- [[chinatown]] — *Chinatown* (1974): genre mastery and reinvention — the Murder Mystery that broke its own convention
- [[the-verdict]] — *The Verdict* (1982): McKee's primary example of character arc — from self-destruction to redemption
- [[kramer-vs-kramer]] — *Kramer vs. Kramer* (1979): textbook inciting incident, spine, and character arc
- [[jaws]] — *Jaws* (1975): escalating points of no return culminating in the obligatory scene
- [[rocky]] — *Rocky* (1976): dual desire — conscious "go the distance," unconscious self-worth
- [[ordinary-people]] — *Ordinary People* (1980): backstory and cast design at work across three levels of conflict
- [[casablanca]] — *Casablanca* (1942): masterful subplot weaving and the paradigmatic obligatory scene
- [[trading-places]] — *Trading Places* (1983): comic scene turn built on a widening gap
- [[wall-street]] — *Wall Street* (1987): moral turning point through choice under pressure
- [[star-wars]] — *Star Wars* (1977): crisis feeding directly into climax
- [[the-empire-strikes-back]] — *The Empire Strikes Back* (1980): climax that keeps reopening the gap
- [[thelma-louise]] — *Thelma & Louise* (1991): delayed crisis detonating into final action
- [[the-deer-hunter]] — *The Deer Hunter* (1978): symbolic ascension and key image
- [[sullivan-travels]] — *Sullivan's Travels* (1941): setup/payoff rewriting the controlling idea
- [[the-terminator]] — *The Terminator* (1984): mythic symbolic escalation through setting

# Notes on Chapter 3: Structure and Setting

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/notes/chapter-03-notes|中文]]

## On the Importance of Setting

The root of all cliché is that the author doesn't understand the world of his story — specifically, he lacks deep understanding of the story's setting and everything it contains. So he "plagiarizes" from similar stories, cobbling together borrowed materials to "construct" his own story.

The setting strictly limits the range of event (structure) choices — you can only choose events within the world the setting establishes.

The first step toward a good story is to start from a small, knowable world. "Small" means your mind can encompass the fictional world you've created; "small" is the precondition for "knowable" — precisely because your mind can encompass what you've created, you must also *understand* it.

## On Research — Writing Things Down

McKee says: "In your head it's only memory, but written down it becomes working knowledge."

**Key insight:** What exists only in your mind is merely memory; only when written down does it become working knowledge. The act of *writing* is crucial. People often have a brilliant idea flash through their minds, are triggered by certain scenes to think certain thoughts, develop insightful reflections based on some content... But if these things stay only in the brain's "RAM" and are never "written" to one's "hard drive," then no matter how beautiful these ideas, insights, and reflections may be, they are merely a fleeting glimpse that may not even leave a memory trace.

## On Inspiration as Cliché

"Because experienced writers never trust so-called inspiration. Inspiration is often only the first idea picked off the top of your head, and sitting on the top of your head is every film you've ever seen, every novel you've ever read, offering clichés to pluck."

**Reflection:** Without accumulation, where does inspiration come from? First ideas are recycled ideas.

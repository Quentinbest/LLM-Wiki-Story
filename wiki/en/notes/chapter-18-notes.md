# Notes on Chapter 18: The Text

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/notes/chapter-18-notes|中文]]

## On Vividness Through Names

- **Nouns name things; verbs name actions.** Vividness on the page comes from naming, not modifying.
- **The more specific, the more vivid.**

## McKee's Passage on Verbs

> "He started to move slowly across the room." How does a person *start* to cross a room on film? He either crosses or he takes a step and stops. And "moves slowly"? "Slowly" is an adverb; "moves" is a vague, washed-out verb. Instead, name the action: "He **pads** across the room." He **ambles, strolls, moseys, saunters, drags himself, staggers, waltzes, glides, lumbers, tiptoes, creeps, slouches, shuffles, waddles, minces, trudges, teeters, lurches, gropes, hobbles** across the room. Each one moves slowly — and each one paints a different, vivid picture.

## Reflection

> Specificity is vividness.

"Slowly" is the writer naming the *quality* of an action. The 19 verbs above each *embody* a different quality of slowness — and a different inner life of the character producing it. Choosing the verb is choosing the character.

## On Titles

> A film's title is a name. An effective title points at something concrete in the story — a character, a setting, a theme, or a genre. The best titles point at two or all of these at once.

The lesson: **titles must be concrete, not abstract.** A concrete title gives the audience an anchor; an abstract one floats.

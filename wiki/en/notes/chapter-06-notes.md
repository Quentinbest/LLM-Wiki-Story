# Notes on Chapter 6: Structure and Meaning

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/notes/chapter-06-notes|中文]]

## On Aesthetic Emotion

McKee defines aesthetic emotion as: "Story is an instrument by which you can create such epiphanies at will."

**Reflection:** Only experiences you've personally lived through, then reflected upon afterward, become meaningful. But art can be meaningful *at the instant it happens*, because it wraps ideas inside emotion. This is also the significance of understanding art — it gives you access to instant meaning that life alone cannot provide.

## On Meaningful Emotional Experience

"In life, experiences become meaningful with reflection in time. In art, they are meaningful now, at the instant they happen."

**Reflection:** Art wraps thought in feeling. That's why understanding art matters — it provides a shortcut to meaning that life requires years of reflection to achieve.

## On Story as Proof

"You want the world to leave your story convinced that yours is a truthful metaphor for life. And the means by which you bring the audience to your point of view resides in the very design you give your telling. As you create your story, you create your proof; idea and structure intertwine in a rhetorical relationship."

**Reflection:** The careful design you make in a story is the evidence for expressing your worldview. Structure is not just craft — it's argument.

## On Show, Don't Tell

"The story's meaning, whether comic or tragic, must be dramatized in an emotionally expressive Story Climax without the aid of explanatory dialogue."

**Reflection:** This is the meaning behind the Western writing maxim "Don't tell, show." The principle extends beyond prose style — it's about how meaning itself must be conveyed through events, not words.

## On Controlling Idea

The Controlling Idea has two components: Value + Cause. The protagonist, because of a primary "Cause," transforms from the opening "Value" (positive/negative) to the final climactic "Value" (negative/positive).

A true theme is: a clear, coherent sentence that expresses the story's irreducible meaning. The Controlling Idea can be expressed in one sentence, describing how and why life changes from one condition at the beginning to another at the end.

## On Self-Recognition

"The Story Climax mirrors your inner self, and if your story is from the very best sources within you, more often than not you'll be shocked by what you see reflected in it."

**Reflection:** The climax reflects who you truly are — often surprising you. If your plot works out exactly as planned, you're not working loosely enough. Writing is discovery.

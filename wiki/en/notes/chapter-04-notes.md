# Notes on Chapter 4: Structure and Genre

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/notes/chapter-04-notes|中文]]

## On Genre Conventions as Boundaries

Genre conventions define the specific settings, characters, events, and values of a genre. Once you determine the genre of your story, you've also drawn the boundaries for these elements within it.

## On Art Film as Intellectual Wrapping

"Art Film favors the intellect by smothering strong emotion under a blanket of mood, while through enigma, symbolism, or unresolved tensions it invites interpretation and analysis."

**Reflection:** What Art Film calls "intellectuality" is really strong emotion wrapped in a gentle blanket of mood, achieved through three means: enigma, symbolism, and unresolved tension. The cerebral quality isn't the absence of emotion — it's emotion made indirect.

## On the Danger of Assuming Knowledge

"Never assume that because you've seen a few films in your genre you already know it. This is like assuming you've heard all of Beethoven's nine symphonies and can now compose one."

**Reflection:** The same logic applies broadly — don't assume that reading a few books means you've understood the principles or knowledge within them. *Knowing* is not the same as being able to *do*.

## On Genre Study Methodology

McKee prescribes a specific method:
1. First, list films similar to yours — both successes and failures. (Studying failures is illuminating... and humbling.)
2. Next, rent the films and if possible buy the screenplays. Study them stop-and-go, breaking each down by setting, character, event, and value.
3. Finally, stack these analyses and look down through them: What do the stories in my genre always do? What are its conventions of time, place, character, and action? Until you find answers, the audience will always be one step ahead.

## On "Positioning the Audience"

We don't want people coming to our film cold and clueless, not knowing what to expect, forcing us to spend the first twenty minutes providing clues to guide them toward the necessary story attitude.

## Industry Terms Noted

- **Word of Mouth:** Personal opinions moviegoers express about a film to potential viewers. Considered the key factor in a film's success after opening weekend.
- **Legs:** A film with strong audience appeal and positive word-of-mouth that ensures a long theatrical run.
- **Sneak Preview:** An advance screening before official release — free, invited, or ticketed — to generate positive word-of-mouth.

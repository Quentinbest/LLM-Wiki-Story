# Notes on Chapter 15: Exposition

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/notes/chapter-15-notes|中文]]

## On the Method: Show, Don't Tell

- **Exposition** — narrative information.
- **Show, don't tell** — never let the audience hear what they should feel through dramatized action.
- **Dramatize** — render exposition as scene-level action under pressure, not as speech.

## A Study Recipe

McKee recommends a single exercise for learning [[exposition-as-ammunition|exposition as ammunition]]: take Oliver Stone's *JFK*, both screenplay and tape. Watch it scene by scene; list every fact, contested or not, that the film must convey. Then map how Stone breaks that Everest of information into indispensable atomic pieces and assembles each piece dramatically — building the investigation step by step.

## Reflection

The lesson is not that *JFK* tells us a great deal. It is that the film never *tells*; it always *delivers*. Each fact is timed, weaponized, and placed where its detonation produces a turn. This is the difference between exposition that bores and exposition that hunts.

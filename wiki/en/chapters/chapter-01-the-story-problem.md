# Chapter 1: The Story Problem

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/chapters/chapter-01-the-story-problem|中文]]

## Summary

McKee opens with a diagnosis: the art of storytelling is in decline. Despite humanity's insatiable appetite for stories—our "equipment for living," as Kenneth Burke puts it—the overall quality of storytelling has eroded. Hollywood produces hundreds of films yearly, but most are mediocre. The hard truth is that what reaches screens represents the best available writing, not studio blindness to hidden genius.

The root cause is a loss of craft. Aspiring writers rush to write without studying the principles of story composition, much as someone might attempt a symphony without studying music theory. Universities have shifted from teaching intrinsic story principles (desire, antagonism, turning points, crisis, climax) to extrinsic literary theory (language, codes, text). The old Hollywood apprenticeship system has vanished. And a deeper erosion of shared values makes storytelling harder than ever.

McKee establishes that story design—not dialogue, not description—is the writer's primary creative task, consuming 75% or more of the labor. He distinguishes between [[story-form]] (the universal, timeless shape of story) and formula (a reductive recipe), insisting writers must grasp form without reducing it. He argues that [[story-as-metaphor]] is the essential understanding: story is not an escape from reality but a vehicle for searching for it.

Finally, McKee distinguishes two talents: literary talent (converting language into expressive form) and story talent (converting life into meaningful experience). The former is common; the latter is rare. Both are needed, but [[craft-maximizes-talent]]—without craft, talent burns wildly but accomplishes nothing.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **[[story-as-metaphor]]** — Story is not escape from reality but a metaphor for life, abstracting essences from the day-to-day
- **[[story-form]]** — The universal, timeless form underlying all stories; form is not formula
- **[[literary-talent-vs-story-talent]]** — Two distinct and unrelated talents required of writers
- **[[craft-maximizes-talent]]** — Craft is the engine that channels talent into storytelling power

## Key Examples

- **[[tender-mercies]]** — Referenced alongside *Raiders of the Lost Ark* as proof that radically different films share universal story form
- Various films cited as examples of story diversity: *Chinatown*, *Hannah and Her Sisters*, *Vertigo*, *8½*, *Rashomon*, *Casablanca*

## McKee's Core Argument

The storytelling crisis is not caused by studio gatekeepers or a lack of talent—it is caused by a loss of craft. Writers must study the principles of story composition as rigorously as a musician studies music theory. The goal is "a good story well told," and achieving this requires mastering [[story-form]], understanding [[story-as-metaphor]], and developing craft to channel talent into art.

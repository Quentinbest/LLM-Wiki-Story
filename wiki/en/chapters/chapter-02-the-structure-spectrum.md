# Chapter 2: The Structure Spectrum

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/chapters/chapter-02-the-structure-spectrum|中文]]

## Summary

McKee introduces the fundamental terminology and architecture of story design. He begins by distinguishing *life story* (the vast totality of a character's existence) from *the story told* (the carefully selected slice the writer chooses to dramatize). The mark of a master is "to select only a few moments but give us a lifetime."

He defines [[structure]] as "a selection of events from the characters' life stories composed into a strategic sequence to arouse specific emotions and to express a specific view of life." He then builds the story hierarchy from the ground up: [[beat]] (the smallest unit—an exchange of action/reaction), [[scene]] (an action through conflict that turns a value), [[sequence]] (a series of scenes culminating in greater impact), [[act]] (a series of sequences peaking in a major reversal), and [[story-climax]] (the final, absolute, irreversible change).

Central to this hierarchy are [[story-values]]—the positive/negative charges of human experience (love/hate, truth/lie, life/death) that shift during events—and the [[story-event]], defined as meaningful change in a character's life expressed in terms of a value and achieved through conflict.

The chapter's second half introduces [[the-story-triangle]], McKee's map of all possible story designs. At the apex sits [[archplot]] (Classical Design): an active protagonist, external conflict, causality, continuous time, consistent reality, closed ending. At the lower-left is [[miniplot]] (minimalism): reduced classical elements—open endings, internal conflict, passive protagonists, multiple protagonists. At the lower-right is [[antiplot]] (antistructure): reversed classical elements—coincidence, nonlinear time, inconsistent realities. McKee analyzes seven formal dimensions across which these three forms differ, and discusses the politics of story design, the shrinking audience as stories move away from Archplot, and the imperative to [[master-classical-form]] before attempting minimalism or antistructure.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **[[structure]]** — A selection of events composed into strategic sequence to arouse emotion and express a view of life
- **[[story-event]]** — Meaningful change in a character's life, expressed through values, achieved through conflict
- **[[story-values]]** — The positive/negative charges of human experience that shift during events
- **[[the-story-triangle]]** — The triangle mapping all story designs: Archplot, Miniplot, Antiplot
- **[[archplot]]** — Classical Design: active protagonist, external conflict, causality, closed ending
- **[[miniplot]]** — Minimalism: open endings, internal conflict, passive or multiple protagonists
- **[[antiplot]]** — Antistructure: coincidence, nonlinear time, inconsistent realities

## Key Examples

- **[[tender-mercies]]** — Exemplar of exquisite plotting within an inner-change story; also cited to refute "plotless" criticism
- *The Rainmaker* — Illustrates complete story event definition (meaningful change through conflict via values)
- *After Hours* — Exemplar Antiplot: coincidence-driven, fragmented narrative
- *Bad Timing* — Antiplot example of nonlinear time expressing "character as destiny"
- *Barton Fink* — Film sitting at center of triangle, blending all three forms
- *Weekend* (Godard) — Antiplot with inconsistent reality (Emily Brontë scene)
- *Paris, Texas* — Miniplot with open ending

## McKee's Core Argument

All stories, despite infinite variety, share a universal architecture built from beats, scenes, sequences, and acts. The writer's task is to compose events—not randomly, but strategically—to create meaningful change. The Story Triangle maps the full universe of story design, with Classical Design (Archplot) as the form most audiences respond to because it mirrors how humans naturally perceive reality. Writers must master classical form before venturing into minimalism or antistructure.

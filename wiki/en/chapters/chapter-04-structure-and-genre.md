# Chapter 4: Structure and Genre

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/chapters/chapter-04-structure-and-genre|中文]]

## Summary

McKee presents genre as the second great structural constraint after setting. He catalogs 25 film genres — from Love Story and Horror to Art Film — arguing that every writer works within genre whether they acknowledge it or not. The audience enters each film as a "genre expert," armed with expectations learned from a lifetime of moviegoing. The writer must fulfill those expectations while leading them to fresh, unexpected moments.

Genre conventions — the specific settings, roles, events, and values that define each genre — are not obstacles but creative catalysts. McKee extends the Principle of [[creative-limitation]] from Chapter 3: just as a poet's rhyme scheme can spark unexpected imagery, genre conventions force the writer to find original solutions within known patterns. The challenge is to "keep convention but avoid cliché."

The chapter examines how genres evolve with society (the Western, the Psycho-Drama, the Love Story each transformed across decades), how genres can be mixed to create new resonances, and how mastery of genre provides the endurance needed for the long marathon of screenwriting. McKee concludes that there is "no necessary contradiction between art and popular success" — Hitchcock proved this definitively.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **[[genre]]** — The 25 film genres and their subgenres, classified by subject, setting, role, event, and values
- **[[genre-conventions]]** — The specific settings, roles, events, and values that define individual genres
- **[[mixing-genres]]** — Combining genres to create richer meaning, character, and emotional variety
- **[[creative-limitation]]** — Extended to genre: conventions are the rhyme scheme of the storyteller's poem

## Key Examples

- **[[chinatown]]** — Broke the Murder Mystery convention by letting the killer go free, reflecting 1970s America's awakening to corruption. A classic that rewrote its genre.
- **Raiders of the Lost Ark** — Fresh execution of the hero-at-the-mercy-of-the-villain convention
- **A Fish Called Wanda** — Illustrates the Comedy convention "nobody gets hurt" through the squashed terrier scene
- **Sleepless in Seattle** — Retooled the Love Story into a "Longing Story" by delaying boy-meets-girl to the climax

## McKee's Core Argument

Genre is not a commercial concern separate from art — it is the structural foundation of all storytelling. The audience is a genre expert; the writer must surpass that expertise. Genre conventions are creative limitations that inspire originality when mastered, and genre evolves with society. The writer who commands genre can guide audiences through rich variations on convention, giving them not only what they hoped for but more than they could have imagined.

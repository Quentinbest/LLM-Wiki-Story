# Creative Choices

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/creative-choices|中文]]

## Definition

Creativity is not one-to-one — devising exactly the events needed and penciling in dialogue. It is five to one, ten to one, even twenty to one. The craft demands inventing far more material than can possibly be used, then making astute selections of quality from this quantity. **Creativity means creative choices of inclusion and exclusion.**

## McKee's Argument

McKee argues that first ideas are almost always clichés. "Inspiration" is typically the first notion grabbed from the top of your head — and sitting there is every film you've seen and novel you've read, offering recycled material to pluck. True inspiration comes from a deeper source, accessed only through disciplined overproduction.

The method: for any given story moment, sketch a list of five, ten, fifteen different versions. Don't write them in full — just bold strokes. Then survey the list asking: Which is truest to character? Truest to world? And has never been onscreen quite this way before? That one goes into the screenplay.

If your finished screenplay contains every scene you've ever written and you've never thrown an idea away, the work will almost certainly fail. "No one has to see your failures unless you add vanity to folly and exhibit them."

## How It Works

1. **Generate abundantly** — For each key scene or moment, brainstorm many variations (5–20+).
2. **Test each against three criteria:** Truest to character? Truest to world? Never been done this way before?
3. **Select the best, burn the rest** — Keep only the top 10% of material.
4. **If the best idea is a cliché,** go deeper — create a new list of variations within that cliché until you find the truth inside it.

## Film Examples

- McKee's hypothetical: writing an East Side Manhattan romantic comedy, the "singles bar meet-cute" is the first idea — a cliché. The writer lists alternatives (tire blowout on Park Avenue, office Christmas party bathroom rescue, etc.), or, if the singles bar truly fits, researches that world until finding the authentic truth inside the cliché.

## Common Mistakes

- Trusting "inspiration" — first ideas are usually borrowed
- Writing only exactly the material needed with no surplus
- Falling in love with a scene and refusing to cut it despite knowing it's mediocre
- Never rewriting beyond dialogue tinkering

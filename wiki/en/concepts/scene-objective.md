# Scene Objective

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/scene-objective|中文]]

## Definition
A **Scene Objective** is the immediate desire a character pursues in the time-and-place of a single scene. It is a local expression of the larger [[spine]].

## McKee's Argument
Scenes stay unified because desire stays specific. However complicated the psychology, someone in the scene wants something now — to win her back, to avoid exposure, to get through the door, to force a confession. The clearer the objective, the clearer the conflict and the sharper the turn.

## How It Works
```mermaid

  spine[Spine]

```

## Film Examples
- **[[casablanca]]** — Rick's local objective in the bazaar scene is to win Ilsa back.
- **[[kramer-vs-kramer]]** — Ted's breakfast objective is simple survival as a father, but it points to a deeper arc.

## Relationship to Other Concepts
- [[scene]] — A scene unifies around a concrete objective.
- [[spine]] — The scene objective should be one facet of the story-long desire.
- [[object-of-desire]] — The scene objective is a tactical reach toward the larger object.
- [[turning-point]] — The objective's failure or mutation creates the turn.

## Common Mistakes
Scenes become vague when writers substitute topic for objective. "They talk about their marriage" is not an objective; "he tries to stop her from leaving" is.

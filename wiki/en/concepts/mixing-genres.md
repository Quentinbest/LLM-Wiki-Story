# Mixing Genres

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/mixing-genres|中文]]

## Definition

Genres are frequently combined to resonate with meaning, enrich character, and create varieties of mood and emotion. A writer in command of genre can cross-breed from over two dozen principal genres, potentially creating a type of film the world has never seen.

## McKee's Argument

Genre mixing is one of the writer's most powerful tools. A Love Story subplot finds its way into almost any Crime Story. The Fisher King wove five threads — Redemption Plot, Psycho-Drama, Love Story, Social Drama, Comedy — into an excellent film. The Musical Horror Film was a "delicious invention." The possibilities for inventive cross-breeding are endless.

Beyond mixing, genres also evolve and reinvent themselves as society changes. The Western transformed from morality plays into quasi-Social Dramas correcting racism. The Psycho-Drama evolved through four stages as society's relationship with insanity changed. The Love Story's blocking forces shifted from parents to culture, identity, and even death. Innovative writers sense where society is leaning and take genres into their next generation.

## How It Works

1. **Identify the primary genre** — This carries the story's main structural spine.
2. **Layer secondary genres** — These enrich character, add emotional variety, and deepen meaning.
3. **Respect each genre's conventions** — Even in a mix, each genre's key conventions must be fulfilled.
4. **Use mixing to create the unexpected** — The combination itself can generate fresh moments neither genre alone could produce.

## Film Examples

- **The Fisher King** — Five genres woven together: Redemption Plot, Psycho-Drama, Love Story, Social Drama, Comedy
- **Chinatown** — Crime/Murder Mystery + Social Drama + Disillusionment Plot
- **West Side Story** — Musical + Social Drama + Love Story

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[genre]] — Mixing requires mastery of multiple genres
- [[genre-conventions]] — Each genre in the mix maintains its own conventions

## Common Mistakes

- Mixing genres without mastering any of them individually
- Letting secondary genres overwhelm the primary story spine
- Ignoring the conventions of secondary genres — the audience still expects them fulfilled

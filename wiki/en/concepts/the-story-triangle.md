# The Story Triangle

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/the-story-triangle|中文]]

## Definition

The Story Triangle is McKee's map of all possible story designs. It is a triangle with [[archplot]] (Classical Design) at the apex, [[miniplot]] (Minimalism) at the lower-left, and [[antiplot]] (Antistructure) at the lower-right. Below the line between Miniplot and Antiplot lies Nonplot—stories that do not arc and remain in stasis. Within this triangle sits "the totality of writers' cosmologies, all their multitudinous visions of reality."

## McKee's Argument

The triangle organizes seven formal dimensions along which stories vary:
1. **Closed vs. Open endings** — Archplot → closed; Miniplot → often open
2. **External vs. Internal conflict** — Archplot → external emphasis; Miniplot → internal emphasis
3. **Single vs. Multiple protagonists** — Archplot → single; Miniplot Multiplot → multiple
4. **Active vs. Passive protagonist** — Archplot → active; Miniplot → relatively passive
5. **Linear vs. Nonlinear time** — Archplot → linear; Antiplot → nonlinear/scrambled
6. **Causality vs. Coincidence** — Archplot → causal chains; Antiplot → random collisions
7. **Consistent vs. Inconsistent realities** — Archplot → consistent; Antiplot → inconsistent

Few films sit at pure extremes—most slide along the spectrums, blending elements. *The Fabulous Baker Boys* falls between Archplot and Miniplot. *Barton Fink* draws from all three corners. *When Harry Met Sally* pushes toward Antiplot with its mockumentary inserts.

McKee also addresses the "politics" of the triangle: Hollywood optimism (Archplot, positive endings) vs. art film pessimism (Nonplot/Miniplot, static/negative). Both extremes distort truth when driven by ideology rather than conviction.

## How It Works

Writers use the triangle to locate their own work and understand their artistic choices. As story design moves away from Archplot toward the lower corners, the audience shrinks—not from lack of quality but because most humans perceive life through classical patterns (causality, linear time, agency, change). The writer must know where they stand and work from genuine belief, not politics.

## Film Examples

- **Center of triangle:** *Barton Fink* — blends all three forms
- **Between Archplot and Miniplot:** *The Fabulous Baker Boys*, *The Crying Game*
- **Nonplot (below the line):** *Umberto D*, *Faces*, *Naked* — life doesn't arc; we gain insight but nothing changes

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[archplot]] — At the apex; the most universal form
- [[miniplot]] — Lower-left; reduces classical elements
- [[antiplot]] — Lower-right; reverses classical elements
- [[structure]] — The triangle maps the full range of structural choices

## Common Mistakes

Treating the triangle as a hierarchy of quality (Archplot ≠ "better"). Also: working in a corner of the triangle for political reasons (to seem "artistic" or "commercial") rather than genuine conviction.

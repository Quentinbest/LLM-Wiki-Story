# Controlling Idea

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/controlling-idea|中文]]

## Definition

The Controlling Idea is a story's irreducible meaning, expressible in a single sentence describing how and why life undergoes change from one condition of existence at the beginning to another at the end. It has two components: **Value** (the positive or negative charge at the last act's climax) plus **Cause** (the chief reason that value has changed to its final state).

## McKee's Argument

"Theme" is too vague. "Poverty," "war," and "love" are not themes — they relate to setting or genre. A true theme is a sentence, not a word. McKee prefers "Controlling Idea" because it names the root idea *and* implies its function: it shapes strategic choices, guiding what is appropriate or inappropriate in the story.

The more beautifully a story is shaped around one clear idea, the more meanings audiences discover. Conversely, the more ideas packed into a story, the more they implode until the film says nothing. The Controlling Idea emerges from the climax — the story tells you its meaning; you do not dictate meaning to the story. This often produces **Self-Recognition**: the climax mirrors the writer's inner self, frequently shocking them with what they discover.

## How It Works

1. **Create the Story Climax** — The final action that excites, moves, and feels complete.
2. **Identify the Value** — What positive or negative charge is brought into the protagonist's world as a result of this climactic action?
3. **Trace the Cause** — Working backward, what is the chief cause deep within character, society, or environment that brought this value into existence?
4. **Compose the sentence** — Value + Cause = Controlling Idea.

**Examples:**
- *Dirty Harry*: "Justice triumphs because the protagonist is more violent than the criminals."
- *Columbo*: "Justice is restored because the protagonist is more clever than the criminal."
- *Chinatown*: "Evil triumphs because it's part of human nature."
- *Groundhog Day*: "Happiness fills our lives when we learn to love unconditionally."

## Film Examples

- **[[chinatown]]** — "Evil triumphs because it's part of human nature." On the surface, the rich get away with murder. More profoundly, it expresses the ubiquity of evil — good and evil as equal parts of human nature.
- **In the Heat of the Night** — "Justice is restored because a perceptive black outsider sees the truth of white perversion."
- **Dangerous Liaisons** — "Hatred destroys us when we fear the opposite sex."

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing theme-as-topic ("war," "love") with Controlling Idea (a complete sentence of Value + Cause)
- Trying to pack multiple ideas into one story — they implode and the film says nothing
- Dictating meaning to the story instead of letting the climax reveal it
- Didacticism — zealously preaching one side without building the power of the Counter-Idea

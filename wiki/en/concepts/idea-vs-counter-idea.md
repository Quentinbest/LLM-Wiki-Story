# Idea vs. Counter-Idea

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/idea-vs-counter-idea|中文]]

## Definition

Progressions build by moving dynamically between the positive and negative charges of the values at stake in the story. The positive Idea and its negative Counter-Idea argue back and forth, sequence by sequence, creating a dramatized dialectical debate. At climax, one wins and becomes the story's [[controlling-idea|Controlling Idea]].

## McKee's Argument

From the moment of inspiration, the writer builds a bridge from Premise to Controlling Idea. The events echo contradictory voices of one theme — positive and negative assertions of the same idea contest through the film, building in intensity, until at Crisis they collide head-on. Out of this collision rises the Story Climax, in which one idea prevails.

This rhythm is fundamental to all fine stories, no matter how internalized the action. It can become very complex, subtle, and ironic — in *Sea of Love*, each scene pointing toward the suspect's guilt turns with irony: positive on justice, negative on love.

## How It Works

Using the Crime Story pattern:
1. **Counter-Idea:** "Crime pays because the criminals are brilliant" — a crime so enigmatic the audience thinks they'll get away with it
2. **Idea:** "Crime doesn't pay because the cop is even more brilliant" — a clue is discovered
3. **Counter-Idea:** The cop is misled — "Crime pays"
4. **Idea:** The real villain is identified — "Crime doesn't pay"
5. **Counter-Idea:** The criminal captures the protagonist — "Crime pays"
6. **Crisis → Climax:** One idea wins decisively

## Film Examples

- **[[chinatown]]** — The negative Counter-Idea wins: "Injustice prevails because the antagonist is overwhelmingly ruthless and powerful"
- **The Silence of the Lambs** — The positive Idea wins: "Justice triumphs because the protagonist is tenaciously resourceful and courageous"

## Common Mistakes

- **Didacticism:** Building only the Idea side and weakening the Counter-Idea — the story becomes a sermon
- **Ambiguity:** Failing to let either side win clearly at climax — irony is clear double-declaration, not blur
- **Coincidence:** Resolving the dialectic through random chance rather than honest motivation

# Genre

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/genre|中文]]

## Definition

Genre is a system for classifying stories by shared elements — subject, setting, role, event, and values. McKee catalogs 25 film genres, ranging from tightly constrained forms (Crime, Horror) to expansive supra-genres (Historical Drama, Animation) that can house any number of basic genres within them.

## McKee's Argument

Every writer is a genre writer, whether they admit it or not. The avant-garde notion of writing "outside the genres" is naive — after thousands of years of storytelling, no story is so different that it bears no resemblance to anything else. Even the Art Film is a genre, with its own conventions of Minimalism and Antistructure.

The audience enters every film as a genre expert, armed with expectations. The writer's challenge is twofold: fulfill those expectations (or risk confusion) while leading them to fresh, unexpected moments (or risk boredom). This is impossible without genre knowledge that surpasses the audience's.

## How It Works

- **Genre determines structure.** Each genre imposes conventions on story design — conventional values at climax, conventional settings, events, and roles.
- **Genre evolves with society.** As social attitudes change, genres transform. The Western, the Psycho-Drama, and the Love Story have all undergone radical evolution across decades.
- **Genres can mix.** Combining genres creates richer meaning and emotional variety. *The Fisher King* wove five genres into one film.
- **Genre provides endurance.** Writing in the genre you love sustains you through the months or years a screenplay demands.

## Common Mistakes

- Believing you can write "outside" genre — you can't; you're just writing in a genre you haven't identified
- Assuming familiarity with a genre equals mastery ("watching Beethoven ≠ composing symphonies")
- Ignoring genre evolution — writing with antiquated conventions that no longer resonate with contemporary audiences
- Dismissing genre as "commercial" concern irrelevant to art

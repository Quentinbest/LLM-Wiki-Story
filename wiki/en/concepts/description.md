# Description

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/description|中文]]

## Definition
**Description** is the non-dialogue prose of a screenplay — the text that puts a film in the reader's head. Written well, it projects the *sensation of watching a film* as the reader turns pages.

## McKee's Argument
Ninety percent of verbal expression has no filmic equivalent. The screenwriter therefore asks relentlessly: **What do I see on the screen?** Then describes only what can be photographed, in the absolute present tense, in vivid motion.

Four rules follow:

- **Vivid nouns and active verbs.** "Spike," not "big nail." "Pads," "saunters," "teeters," "shuffles" — not "moves slowly."
- **Eliminate "is/are."** On screen nothing is in a state of being; everything is becoming.
- **Strike metaphors that cannot be photographed.** "As if" does not exist on screen.
- **Cut "we see" / "we hear"** and most camera notations. The modern screenplay is a Master Scene document.

## How It Works
- **Filmic test.** Every line: what do we see or hear on screen? Cut the unfilmable.
- **Verb for the action.** Replace weak verbs + adverbs with a single precise verb.
- **Name the thing.** Concrete nouns beat adjectives-plus-generics.
- **Master Scene discipline.** Include only the angles the story requires. Use paragraph breaks to *suggest* angles — wide, moving, close-up — without labeling them.
- **Eliminate CUT TO / SMASH CUT TO / LAP DISSOLVE TO** and similar.
- **No camera direction.** Actors ignore behavioral micromanagement and directors ignore RACK FOCUS TO; both break the illusion.

## Film Examples
- Screenplays of Lean, Bergman, Towne, the Coens read well because their description projects the film.
- Counter-example: European submissions cited by McKee ("The sun sets like a tiger's eye closing in the jungle") — beautiful prose, unphotographable.
- American counter-example: sarcasm used as description ("You guessed it. Here comes the sex scene…") — cleverness masking inability to write the scene.

## Relationship to Other Concepts
- Outranks [[dialogue]] in the [[silent-screenplay]] doctrine.
- Hosts the first and largest placements of [[image-systems]].
- Collaborates with actor and director; leaves them room.

## Common Mistakes
- Unfilmable metaphor or simile.
- Adverb + generic verb instead of a specific verb.
- Camera notations that try to direct the film on the page.
- Over-described behaviors that crowd out the actor.
- "We see" / "we hear" breaking the reader's immersion.

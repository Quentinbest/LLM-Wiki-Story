# Research

> 中文版：[[wiki/zh/concepts/research|中文]]

## Definition

Research is the systematic acquisition of knowledge about a story's world. McKee identifies three methods: **research of memory** (mining personal experience), **research of imagination** (vivid hypothetical exploration of characters' lives), and **research of fact** (library and field study). A story generally needs all three.

## McKee's Argument

Research is the key to winning the war on cliché. The writer who lacks deep knowledge of the story's world will inevitably recycle material from other works. More practically, research is also the cure for writer's block: "You're blocked because you have nothing to say. Your talent didn't abandon you… you can't kill your talent, but you can starve it into a coma through ignorance."

McKee warns that research must not become procrastination. It is "meat to feed the beasts of imagination and invention, never an end in itself." Creation and investigation go back and forth — they are not sequential phases but an alternating process. The moment when a story seems to "write itself" is simply the point where the writer's knowledge has reached saturation.

## How It Works

1. **Memory** — Ask: "What do I know from personal experience that touches on my characters' lives?" Relive past experiences in vivid detail and *write them down*. "In your head it's only memory, but written down it becomes working knowledge."
2. **Imagination** — Ask: "What would it be like to live my character's life hour by hour?" Sketch scenes — shopping, praying, making love — that may never appear in the story but draw you into the imagined world until it feels like déjà vu.
3. **Fact** — Go to the library. Read respected works related to your story's domain. Two things happen: (a) your personal experience is confirmed as universal, meaning you'll have an audience; (b) your knowledge expands beyond your finite circle of experience with powerful new insights.

## Common Mistakes

- Believing you know enough about a subject from casual experience
- Letting research become procrastination — spending years studying without writing
- Treating research as information gathering rather than material for event creation
- Keeping insights in your head rather than writing them down
